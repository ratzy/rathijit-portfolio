The question nobody could answer

Haleon's MEA marketing teams ran omnichannel campaigns to healthcare professionals — reps, email, webinars, WhatsApp, events. Millions in spend. Dozens of touchpoints. And one question they couldn't answer:

Is our channel strategy actually working?

They had dashboards. They had data. They didn't have an answer.

Why it was broken

  • Data was fragmented. Analysts hand-stitched the old dashboard, Google Analytics, IPSOS and search reports — days of work, obsolete on arrival.
  • KPIs sat in silos. You could see email opens. You could see webinar attendance. You couldn't see whether one caused the other.
  • Activity never reached outcomes. So every budget conversation ran on instinct, not evidence.
  • There was no project brief. I wrote one — scope, phases, KPIs as Jira tickets, milestones — before designing anything.

Finding the real mental model

I didn't ask stakeholders what they wanted on a dashboard. That produces feature lists. I asked them to think out loud instead:

Imagine campaign ROI dropped last month. Walk me through how you'd figure out why.

Every action and KPI they named went on a sticky note, left to right, across the whiteboard. People are bad at describing what they need — and excellent at describing how they think. That decision flow became the information architecture.

Two people, two altitudes

Interviews surfaced two personas — and the gap between them shaped the whole product. Leadership needed to know whether it worked. The Omnichannel Activation Lead needed to know why, and what to do next.

The hypothesis

If stakeholders see the HCP journey as a sequence rather than scattered metrics, they can find where engagement leaks — and act.

Everything after this was an attempt to prove or break that.

The solution — a funnel, first

Awareness → Consideration → Intent → Conversion → Retention. Each stage carrying its own objectives, metrics and decision levers. Why a funnel? It gave both personas a shared language. Before, they described the same problem in different vocabularies. Now they could argue about where the drop-off was — not whether there was one.

The third view is the one that mattered. The first two make people better analysts. The third makes them faster deciders — surfacing recommendations instead of reporting history. That's not a feature. It's a change in how the team operates: hindsight → continuous optimisation.

Prototyping before building

Power BI development is slow and expensive to change. Every wrong assumption we shipped into dev cost weeks. So I prototyped the interactions in Topaz Fabric Studio with AI-assisted vibe coding — letting stakeholders use the thing before it was built, and validating complex data views before Power BI development started.

Working inside the constraint

Power BI isn't a blank canvas. Several patterns I wanted weren't natively supported. Rather than design something beautiful and unbuildable, I brought developers into the design conversation early — feasibility checks, KPI accuracy validation, end-to-end testing. Then we A/B tested with stakeholders during UAT. Not do you like it — can you find the answer?

What changed for Haleon

A funnel-first strategy gave marketing a shared language for performance. One dashboard replaced 3+ manual reporting sources. Leadership gained go/no-go capability without ad-hoc data pulls. And the Suggestion Dashboard moved them from hindsight to continuous optimisation.

What I'd do differently

I shipped this without instrumenting it. I have adoption. I have UAT validation. I don't have a 90-day read on whether decision speed actually improved, or whether a single budget moved because of the Suggestion view.

I'd negotiate the success metric at kickoff now, and hold both sides to it. The design was rigorous. The proof wasn't designed with the same rigour. That's the discipline I'm bringing forward.
