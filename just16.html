Fun is a design constraint

Most enterprise design optimises for efficiency. This one optimised for willingness — because a survey nobody finishes produces no data, no matter how well-structured the questions are.

The problem

[24]7.ai's employee feedback process was lengthy and cumbersome. Participation was low — which meant the company couldn't gather the insights it needed to actually improve the workplace. A feedback loop broken at the first step.

16 questions × 4 options each — layered with detailed sub-options. That's not a survey. That's an interrogation.

The business question: how do we make feedback fast enough that people actually give it — so the organisation can make data-driven decisions on work-life balance, communication channels and internal resources?

The real problem: feedback fatigue

We named it early, because naming it changed what we were solving. The issue was never the questions — the questions were fine. The issue was the experience of being asked — a tedious drill-down that signalled this will take forever before you'd answered one.

So the design problem became: how do you make an obligation feel like a moment?

Move 01 — Break the mountain into hills

We implemented a categorised feedback system. Each question offered four distinct answer types with clear options — structured enough to analyse responses and identify trends, small enough that no single screen felt like work.

Move 02 — Give the answers a face

We collaborated with an illustrator and animator to build a fun, energetic interface — with character animations tied to each answer choice. Why characters, not copy: an abstract statement invites an abstract answer. A character visibly living that answer invites recognition. People stopped evaluating a sentence and started picking the version of the office they knew.

That's the whole trick — the relatable character did the emotional translation the words couldn't.

Move 03 — Celebrate the small win

Badges awarded for completing sets of questions — acknowledging small achievements, building a sense of accomplishment. Gamified, and effective at motivating employees to progress further and complete the entire form.

Why mid-flow, not end-of-flow: a reward at the finish only motivates people already near it. A reward every few questions gives you a reason to take the next step — which is the only step that ever matters in a drop-off problem.

Trust, made visible

We fostered honest feedback by putting transparent progress indicators and readily accessible badges inside the flow. Employees could track progress throughout, seeing completed sections and the badges still available.

The logic: people give guarded answers when they don't know how much more is coming. Showing the end makes the middle safe. Transparency wasn't a nicety — it was what made honesty possible.

Mobile-first, in 2017

We designed for the small screen first and scaled up — before that was standard practice. The constraint that forced invention: screen-size limits made the existing interaction model impossible on phones. So we built a time-selection behaviour for phones, keeping the experience smooth and intuitive across every platform.

What I did

Creative direction — driving the concept, working with the illustrator and animator, and shaping an approach grounded in a deep understanding of user behaviour. Built in VS Code. Managed in Asana. Visuals in Photoshop.

What changed

A feedback form became a feedback experience. Employees stopped enduring the survey and started finishing it — which meant [24]7.ai finally got the sentiment data it had been asking for all along.

What I'd do differently

“Significantly increased” is the most honest thing I can say — and it isn't good enough. I know participation rose. I don't know from what, to what. We never baselined completion rate before launch, so the thing we set out to fix is the one number I can't show you.

I'd also research the badges properly. Gamification is easy to believe in and hard to prove — I never isolated whether the badges drove completion, or whether the characters did all the work and the badges just came along for the ride.
