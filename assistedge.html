Case study · Healthcare & Insurance · Service

AssistEdge

Insurance customer support · Senior Lead Design Partner · Team of 4

Rebuilding the agent's day, then shipping the code.

Role
Senior Lead Design Partner & Developer
Team
4 designers led and mentored
Delivery
Production HTML/CSS · working prototype
Focus
Research · Journey · Full-stack design

The invisible user

Insurance customers call, chat and video their support agents about policies and claims. The customer's experience of that call is entirely determined by someone the company had stopped designing for.

The agent.

Why it was broken

The call centre application was complex and unintuitive. Agents spent significant time just navigating it. Task flow was unclear. Managing claims and upselling policies — the two things the business actually measured them on — were both harder than they needed to be.

The compounding cost: every second an agent hunts for information is a second the customer waits. The interface wasn't just an internal inefficiency — it was the customer experience.

Research: not one journey, many

Research played an unusually central role here — because there was no single agent journey to map. We collected multiple caller scenarios and prioritised tasks accordingly.

What the taxonomy revealed: agents weren't doing one job. They were doing six — reimbursement follow-ups, emergency hospitalisation approvals, policy servicing, premium queries, new policy sales, document requests — each with a different information need and a different urgency.

The old app treated all six identically. That was the bug.

The persona behind the queue

We built a persona from the scenarios — a call-centre agent, low-to-medium tech expertise, judged on handle time and resolution.

Not getting access to customer information quickly.

The line that mattered most. Everything else was downstream of that.

Ideation: thinking out loud, together

We used digital whiteboarding to onboard the team, drive discussion, and understand how agents actually think through a call.

I mentored the team of 4 through this — briefing them, keeping everyone on the same page, and translating stakeholder criteria into design direction.

The approach

A five-phase arc, sized to the problem.

Why the shape matters: six weeks of development against two of testing meant one thing — the design had to be right before dev started, and legible enough for engineers to build without guessing. That constraint drove the biggest decision on the project.

The decision: ship the code, not the spec

We didn't hand over a spec. We handed over working HTML. I designed the interface and the team built the clickable, production-ready front-end — then shared a working prototype with the client.

Why this was the right call: a spec is a description of a thing. Code is the thing. Every translation step between them is a place for intent to leak — and with six weeks of dev and no room for rework, leakage was the risk. So we removed the translation step.

The second reason — the one I care about more: this became a teaching mechanism. Annotating design directly to markup — headings, labels, inputs, buttons, error states, containers — taught the designers on my team to think in structure, not just surface. They stopped designing pictures of interfaces and started designing interfaces.

That's how I mentor: not lectures. Artefacts that make the lesson unavoidable.

From wireframe to visual

The rebuilt agent view brought everything the persona needed into one surface — policies, claims, appeals, customer profile, sentiment, expiry alerts — ordered by how agents actually work a call, not by how the data was structured.

Collaboration

Regular communication with the team on blockers and work plans. Digital whiteboards for collaboration and comments. Retrospective calls to improve future planning.

Outcomes

What changed

The agent stopped navigating and started resolving. Six call types that had been flattened into one generic flow each got a path that matched how the work actually happens. And the design reached engineering without a translation layer.

What I'd do differently

I never saw the numbers. We designed to reduce navigation time and clarify task flow. I believe it did. I can't prove it — because we didn't instrument handle time, resolution rate or upsell conversion before or after.

The irony isn't lost on me: I designed a dashboard persona who lives and dies by their metrics, and I didn't define my own. I'd baseline handle time at kickoff now and read it at 90 days. The design was validated. The impact was assumed.
